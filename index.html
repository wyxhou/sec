UNIT I 5 hrs

Introduction to Entrepreneurship

Definitions, traits, types, and the role entrepreneurs play in economic growth.

Core Definitions Part A

An innovator who carries out new combinations to initiate economic development — through new products, new markets, new sources of raw materials, and new forms of organisation. — J. A. Schumpeter
A person who always searches for change, responds to it, and exploits it as an opportunity. Innovation is the basic tool of entrepreneurs. — Peter Drucker
★ Etymology Alert: "Entrepreneur" comes from the French word ENTREPRENDRE (to undertake). This is a very common 2-mark question.
Entrepreneurship: The dynamic process of vision, change, and creation — requiring energy and passion toward implementing new ideas and creative solutions.
Enterprise: The business organisation or activity itself. A framework within which decisions are made, resources deployed, and value created continuously.
🧠 Think of it like this
The Entrepreneur is the chef, Entrepreneurship is the act of cooking, and the Enterprise is the restaurant. One person, one process, one outcome — but three distinct concepts.

Functions of an Entrepreneur Part B High Priority

⚠️ Note: Characteristics ≠ Functions. These are different topics — a common exam trap. Functions describe what an entrepreneur does; characteristics describe what they are. Sometimes asked as a case study.

Core Functions

  • Innovation: Introducing new products, services, methods, or markets. Schumpeter called this "creative destruction."
  • Risk-Bearing: Willingly taking on financial, social, and psychological uncertainty of a new venture.
  • Organising Resources: Combining land, labour, capital, and technology into a productive enterprise.
  • Decision-Making: Making key strategic choices about production, pricing, markets, and investments.
  • Management & Coordination: Planning, organising, directing, and controlling business operations.
  • Marketing: Identifying customer needs and ensuring the product reaches the right people at the right price.
  • Capital Formation: Mobilising funds from personal savings, banks, investors, or government schemes.
  • Employment Creation: Creating jobs directly (within the firm) and indirectly (through the supply chain).
⚠️ Exam Tip: If asked as a case study, identify which function the entrepreneur in the scenario is performing — e.g., Deepinder Goyal pivoting Zomato during COVID = Decision-making + Innovation.

Role of Entrepreneurs in Economic Growth Part B

  • Innovator: Disrupts stagnant markets with new ideas. Jio disrupted telecom, reducing data prices by 95% overnight.
  • Employment Generator: MSMEs alone provide 110 million jobs in India (45% of manufacturing output).
  • Economic Growth: Contributes to GDP; 2025 saw 44,000 new startup registrations in India.
  • Social Stability: Reduces poverty, bridges income gaps — seen through micro-enterprises in tier-2/3 cities.
  • Balanced Regional Development: MSME clusters in Tiruppur (textiles), Moradabad (brassware), Rajkot (engineering).
  • Export Promotion: MSME products represent 45.73% of India's total exports (2023-24).
  • Import Substitution: Generic medicines by Sun Pharma, Indian semiconductors (under ₹76,000 crore PLI scheme).

Serial: starts businesses sequentially, often sells each one. Portfolio: owns and runs multiple businesses simultaneously without selling. Example — Kunal Shah (serial), Ratan Tata (portfolio).

🧠 The Idea vs Opportunity Test
"I should make an app for finding parking" = Idea.  "10 million urban commuters spend 18 minutes daily finding parking, no dominant solution exists, municipalities are willing to partner" = Opportunity. The difference is evidence.

Sources of Business Ideas Part A

  • Human Needs & Wants: Dunzo (hyperlocal delivery), Zepto (10-minute grocery — born from a student's frustration with slow delivery)
  • Solving Social Problems: SugarFit (reversing diabetes via lifestyle), iKure (rural healthcare via satellite clinics)
  • Improving Existing Products: Rebel Foods turned dark kitchens into a ₹900 crore empire (Faasos, Behrouz Biryani, 8 other brands, same kitchen)
  • Extending Models to New Sectors: Awfis brought WeWork's co-working model to tier-2 cities like Jaipur & Lucknow
  • Commercialising Hobbies: Vahdam Teas — a tea enthusiast built a direct-to-consumer brand now exporting to 100+ countries
  • Demand-Supply Gaps: Bamboo India (bamboo toothbrushes), Tvasta Manufacturing (3D-printed affordable housing)

Opportunity Analysis — Two Stages Part B High Priority

🔴 Two stages of opportunity selection is a highlighted red topic. Know the names, definitions, and sub-types (Market-Driven vs People-Driven) clearly.
The overwhelming majority of successful innovations exploit change. — Peter F. Drucker

Stage 1: Opportunity Sighting

  • Market Driven — Gaps in existing markets (e.g., no good organic baby food brand in India → Slurrp Farm)
  • People Driven — Leveraging personal skill/passion (e.g., a doctor starting Practo after struggling with paper records)
  • Triggered by: new technology, regulation change, demographic shift, social disruption

Stage 2: Opportunity Evaluation

  • Filter through Opportunity Evaluation Sieve
  • 3 perspectives: Entrepreneur, Customer, Industry
  • Output: Opportunity Map — the foundation of your business model
3 Evaluation Perspectives
1
Entrepreneur's PerspectiveDoes it align with my passion, values, risk appetite, and skills? Do I have an unfair advantage here? Example: Kiran Mazumdar-Shaw had a science background — she could evaluate biotech more accurately than a generalist.
2
Customer / Market PerspectiveIs there a real, large-enough problem being solved? Is the value proposition exciting? Cultural and socioeconomic alignment? Example: Meesho understood that women in tier-3 India needed zero-investment reselling — cultural insight drove explosive growth.
3
Industry / Business PerspectiveHow complex is entry? What is the competitive landscape? What is your sustainable competitive advantage (USP)? Capital requirements? Example: PharmEasy evaluated high fragmentation in pharmacy retail as a structural opportunity, not a threat.

What Makes a Good Opportunity? Part A

  • Solves a real, painful problem — not a nice-to-have
  • Large enough customer base to sustain a viable business
  • Minimal regulation barriers or no existing monopoly
  • Ability to differentiate from competition and maintain that advantage
⚠️ How Good Ideas Go Bad: Novice entrepreneurs fall in love with their idea; experienced ones fall in love with the market. If the market is wrong, no idea can save you.

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit II

Q: What are the two stages of opportunity analysis?
(1) Opportunity Sighting — spotting the gap (market-driven or people-driven). (2) Opportunity Evaluation — testing via the Evaluation Sieve across 3 perspectives.
Q: Distinguish between Market-Driven and People-Driven opportunities.
Market-Driven: identified from external gaps in demand/supply (e.g., Zepto for fast grocery). People-Driven: created from entrepreneur's personal skills/experiences (e.g., a former nurse launching a MedTech startup).
UNIT III 5 hrs

Business Models & Business Creation

Types of business models, entity structures, company formation steps, and government schemes.

Business Model — Meaning Part A

A business model is the strategy a company uses to generate revenue. It describes how an organisation creates, delivers, and captures value. A well-designed business model answers: What do we sell? Who pays us? How do we keep costs lower than our income?

Types of Business Models Part C

Learn all 11 types. Hover each card for a quick jog.

🎯
1. Niche
Targets a small, highly specific segment with unique needs. Charges premium because competitors are too broad to serve them well. Depth over breadth.
The Moms Co. (toxin-free baby care) Blue Tokai (specialty coffee) Vegan Blue Shoes
📊
2. Long Tail
Coined by Chris Anderson (2004). Sell a huge variety of items in small quantities each. The "tail" of niche products collectively rivals bestsellers — but only at internet scale.
AmazonNetflixSpotify Craftsvilla (ethnic artisan products)
🔧
3. Mass Customisation
Combines mass production efficiency with individual customisation. Factory-scale cost, personal-scale experience.
Nike By You Titan (engraved watches) FabIndia (customisable kurtas)
🆓
4. Freemium
Basic version free. Advanced features cost. Four subtypes: Feature Limited (Canva), Time Limited (old Netflix trial), Capacity Limited (Dropbox 2 GB free), Customer Class Limited (student vs enterprise).
Zoho (full CRM free up to 3 users) SpotifyLinkedIn
✂️
5. Unbundling
Break one product into standalone parts. Customers pay only for what they use. Increases revenue per customer while appearing cheaper upfront.
Tata Sky / Tata Play (channel à la carte) IndiGo (base fare + baggage + meal separate)
📦
6. Bundling
Offer multiple products as a combined deal. Works when marginal cost of bundling is low. Customers feel they get more value; company increases average order value.
McDonald's Meal Combos Microsoft Office Suite JioFiber + JioTV + JioCinema bundle
🏷️
7. No Frills
Strip away everything non-essential. Own properties (no rent risk), bulk buy directly from manufacturers, minimal décor, consistent everyday low prices. Attracts price-sensitive, high-frequency buyers.
DMart (India's most profitable retailer per sq. ft.) Udupi restaurants
💎
8. Premium
High-end products for affluent consumers. Brand image is the product. Lower volume, higher margin. Emotional value often exceeds functional value.
Tanishq (gold jewellery) Forest Essentials (luxury Ayurveda) Rolls-Royce, Rolex, Gucci
🔓
9. Open Business Model
Invite external collaborators — customers, developers, universities — to co-create value. Reduces R&D cost, taps wider talent, builds community loyalty.
Threadless (user-designed T-shirts) Flipkart Seller Hub (open to all SMEs) Wikipedia
🔗
10. Multi-Sided Platform (MSP)
Connects two or more interdependent user groups. Value grows with users — this is the Network Effect. The platform itself produces nothing; it enables transactions.
Swiggy (restaurants ↔ customers ↔ delivery partners) Urban Company (professionals ↔ customers) Uber / Ola
🌱
11. Social Enterprise
Business logic applied to social problems. Revenue is a means, not the end. Sustainability matters, but mission comes first.
Amul (dairy cooperative) Goonj (disaster relief + livelihood) Mann Deshi Foundation (rural women's bank)
★ Lean Canvas (for Part C): One-page plan focusing on Problem → Customer Segments → Unique Value Prop → Solution → Channels → Revenue Streams → Cost Structure → Key Metrics. Uber's canvas: Problem = taxis expensive & unreliable. UVP = "Tap a button, get a ride."

Forms of Business Organisation Part C High Priority

🔴 Red-highlighted in important topics. Focus especially on Sole Proprietorship, Joint Stock Company, and LLC — including advantages, disadvantages, and liability type.
Sole Proprietorship Unlimited Liability
One owner; not a separate legal entity. Owner IS the business — all profits and all risks.
✓ Cheap setup, few regulations, pay individual tax (no corp tax)
✗ Unlimited liability, hard to raise funds, short business life
e.g. A local kirana store owner
Partnership Unlimited Liability
2+ people pool capital and skills. Dissolves when ownership changes (partner leaves/dies).
✓ More capital, reduced tax, combined skills
✗ Unlimited liability, limit on partners, unstable
e.g. Law firms, CA firms
Joint-Stock Company Limited Liability
Separate legal entity; owned by shareholders. Can own property, sue, be sued. Shares freely transferable.
e.g. Reliance Industries Ltd. (shares on BSE/NSE)
LLC / Pvt Ltd Limited Liability
Owners = Members. Liability capped at investment. Ownership by % — transfer requires member consent.
e.g. Flipkart Pvt Ltd (before IPO considerations)
Cooperative Limited Liability
Voluntary, member-owned. Philosophy: "all for each and each for all." Democratic — 1 member = 1 vote regardless of capital. Surplus shared by contribution, not capital.
e.g. Amul (3.6 million dairy farmers, ₹72,000 crore turnover) · IFFCO (fertiliser cooperative)
⚠️ LLC vs Joint-Stock Company: Key exam distinction — LLC ownership is in % (hard to transfer, needs consent); Joint-Stock ownership is via freely tradeable shares. JStC suits large public companies; LLC suits small-medium private firms.

Organisational Plan — Recruitment, Training, Motivation & Retention Part B

📌 These sub-topics from the Organisational Plan module are listed in the important topics — each could appear as a 2-mark or 5-mark question.
Recruitment
Recruitment: "The process of locating, identifying and attracting capable applicants to an organisation." — It is the first step in staffing.

Objectives

  • Find the right person for each role
  • Build a pipeline of qualified candidates
  • Reduce hiring time and cost
  • Ensure diversity and inclusion

Sources of Recruitment

  • Internal: Promotions, transfers, employee referrals
  • External: Job portals (Naukri, LinkedIn), campus placements, newspaper ads, recruitment agencies
Training
Training: "The planned and organised activity to impart skills, techniques and methodologies to employees for contributing to the performance and success of the organisation."

Objectives of Training

  • Increase quantity and quality of output
  • Train for promotion to higher jobs
  • Adopt and use advanced technology
  • Handle organisational changes
  • Train in cultural values of the organisation

Training Methods

  • On-the-Job: Induction, Apprenticeship, Job Rotation, Coaching, Job Instruction
  • Off-the-Job: Lectures, Seminars & Conferences, Role Playing, Audio-Visual Training
Motivation
Motivation: The set of forces that cause people to behave in certain ways. Process: Needs → Interpretation of Goals → Goal-Directed Behaviour → Accomplishment.

Internal (Pull) Factors

  • Desire to do something new
  • Educational qualification
  • Technical background
  • Years of experience
  • Occupational background

External (Push) Factors

  • Government support & schemes
  • Availability of resources
  • Promising demand for the product
  • Support from business houses / mentors
Employee Retention
Employee Retention: A systematic effort by employers to create and foster an environment that encourages current employees to remain employed by having policies and practices that address their diverse needs.

Causes of Turnover

  • Cultural conflict
  • Poor work environment
  • Long commuting
  • Lack of appreciation
  • Lack of organisational support
  • No career progression

Retention Strategies

  • Attractive compensation & fringe benefits
  • Sound recognition programmes
  • Personal development plans
  • Appropriate working conditions
  • Participative management
  • Equal opportunity practices

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit III

Q: What is the Long Tail model? Who coined it?
Coined by Chris Anderson (2004). Selling large numbers of unique niche items in small quantities each. The combined sales of the "tail" rivals bestsellers. Examples: Amazon, Netflix, Craftsvilla.
Q: Difference between Freemium and Premium models?
Freemium = free basic + paid advanced features (Zoho, Spotify, Canva). Premium = exclusively high-price offering with no free tier, targeting affluent segments (Forest Essentials, Rolex). Completely opposite strategies.
Q: What is the Network Effect in MSPs?
As user count grows, the platform becomes more valuable for ALL users. Swiggy: more restaurants → better choice for customers → more customers → more orders for restaurants. A virtuous cycle.

Marketing Plan — 10 Components Part C High Priority

🔴 Market survey techniques and Marketing mix – 7 Ps are both red-highlighted. Learn all 10 components + the 7 Ps and market survey techniques as separate sub-topics.
A marketing plan is a written statement of marketing objectives, strategies, and activities to be followed in the business plan.
Step 1
Market Research
Collect and analyse data: who needs your product, competitor behaviour, buying patterns, industry benchmarks. AMA defines MR as "systematic gathering, recording and analysing of data about marketing problems."
Step 2
Target Market & Segmentation
Identify where your product fits and who underserved customers are. Find the gap competitors are missing. Example: Mamaearth targeted parents of young children wanting "toxin-free" baby products — a segment multinational FMCG brands ignored.
Step 3
Product / Service Strategy
Define the product's features, market fit, and added value above customer expectations. How does it do more than what customers currently use?
Step 4
Competition Analysis
Develop your USP (Unique Selling Proposition) — articulated as a "unique customer value." What do you do better? Where are the gaps? Example: Zepto's USP = 10-minute delivery, vs Blinkit's 30-minute standard.
Step 5
Overall Business Strategy
A crisp statement: who you sell to, what you sell, and what makes you distinct. Test it against every part of your business.
Step 6
Market Strategies
Partnering & networking, external communications (advertising, PR, promotions), internal comms, training programmes.
Step 7
Pricing, Positioning & Branding
Determine price strategy aligned with your positioning. Premium (Forest Essentials) vs value (DMart) — your price signals your position.
Step 8
Budget
Match strategies to available budget. In-house vs outsource. Startups often under-invest in marketing — budget forces prioritisation.
Step 9
Marketing Goals
Quantifiable targets: e.g., 30 new clients/month, 30% revenue growth, NPS above 60. Always numbers-based.
Step 10
Monitor Results
Google Analytics, customer surveys, online polls, CRM data. Track → analyse → adjust. Marketing is iterative, not one-and-done.
🔴 7 Ps of Marketing Mix — Red Highlighted: Product · Price · Place · Promotion + Physical Evidence · People · Process. The last 3 distinguish service marketing from product marketing — know specific definitions.
🔴 Market Survey Techniques — Red Highlighted

Techniques of Market Survey

  • Observation: Study consumer behaviour without direct interaction — capturing real-time actions and emotional responses. No interviews needed; used in stores, online behaviour analytics.
  • Experimentation: Deliberately manipulate one variable and measure its effect on another. E.g., A/B testing two different ad copies to see which drives more clicks.
  • Depth Interviewing: Questions administered in a relaxed environment where respondents express themselves freely without fear of disapproval. Uncovers honest opinions.
  • Projective Techniques: Uncover hidden emotions and subconscious attitudes by having respondents project onto ambiguous stimuli. E.g., "Complete this sentence: When I shop at DMart, I feel ___."
  • Survey / Questionnaire: Structured collection of data from a sample of respondents. Most common primary data tool. Can be online, phone, or in-person.
  • Focus Groups: Small group discussion (6–10 people) moderated to gather qualitative insights on a product, brand, or concept.

Financial Plan Part C High Priority

🧠 Balance Sheet Analogy
Think of it as a personal financial photo. What you own (assets) minus what you owe (liabilities) = your net worth. A company's net worth works exactly the same way — but photographed on a specific date.
Break Even Analysis
BEP = the point where Total Revenue = Total Costs. Also called the No Profit, No Loss Zone. Below BEP = loss; above BEP = profit.
BEP (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
☕
BEP in Practice
Blue Tokai Café, Bengaluru
Fixed costs: rent ₹80,000 + staff ₹1,20,000 = ₹2,00,000/month. Each cup priced at ₹280, variable cost ₹80. Contribution = ₹200/cup. BEP = 2,00,000 ÷ 200 = 1,000 cups/month. Below that = loss; every cup above = pure contribution to profit.
Ratio Analysis (Low Priority — know meanings only)
Liquidity RatiosCan the firm pay its bills this year? (Current Ratio, Quick Ratio)
Solvency RatiosCan the firm survive long-term debt? (Debt-to-Equity, Interest Coverage)
Activity RatiosHow efficiently are assets being used? (Inventory Turnover, Asset Turnover)
Profitability RatiosIs the firm generating value? (Net Profit Margin, ROE, ROCE)

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit IV

Q: What is the Break Even Point? State the formula.
BEP = point where Total Revenue = Total Costs (no profit, no loss). Formula: BEP (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit).
Q: Distinguish between MOA and AOA.
MOA = constitution of the company; governs company-outsider relationship; has 6 mandatory clauses. AOA = internal rulebook; governs company-member relationships; covers shares, directors, meetings, winding up.
Q: List the 7 Ps of the Marketing Mix.
Product, Price, Place (Distribution), Promotion + Physical Evidence, People, Process. First 4 apply to products; all 7 apply to service businesses.

Growth Strategies Part B Very Important

🔴 "Main strategies of growth – very important" per the important topics sheet. Know both Internal and External growth strategies with clear distinctions and Indian examples.

🏠 Internal Growth

  • Market Penetration — Sell more of same product to same market (Jio: more data, same India)
  • Market Development — Same product, new market (Amul entering USA)
  • Product Development — New products for existing customers (Amul adding ice cream, pizza to dairy base)
  • Diversification — New products in new markets (ITC: from cigarettes to FMCG to hotels)

🤝 External Growth

  • Joint Venture — Temporary partnership for a specific goal. Profits shared in agreed ratio. (Maruti + Suzuki)
  • Merger — Absorption (one absorbs another) or Amalgamation (both form new entity). (HDFC + HDFC Bank)
  • Sub-Contracting — Assign part of work to another firm. (Zomato contracts logistics to third parties)
  • Franchising — License trademark + systems. Franchisee pays fee + royalty. (Domino's India = Jubilant FoodWorks as master franchisee)
Merger distinction: Absorption = one company takes over another; acquired company loses identity. Amalgamation = both companies dissolve, forming a brand new entity. Example: HDFC Bank + HDFC Ltd merger (2023) was an Absorption — HDFC Ltd merged into HDFC Bank.

Exit Strategies Part B Very Important

🔴 Explicitly flagged as "very important" in the PDF in red. Know all 5 exit strategies + 7 reasons for exiting + how to prepare for an exit. This is a likely Part B or Part C question.
  • Merger or Acquisition (M&A): Sell business to another company. Walmart acquired Flipkart for $16B. Zomato acquired Blinkit (then Grofers).
  • Management / Investor Buyout: Partners or employees buy out the founder. Common in family businesses transitioning to professional management.
  • IPO (Initial Public Offering): List shares on stock exchange. Founder dilutes stake for liquidity. Nykaa, Zomato, Paytm all used IPO as exit path.
  • Liquidation: Close and sell assets. Used when business cannot be salvaged. Kingfisher Airlines (2012) is a famous Indian example.
  • Family/Successor Handover: Transfer to heirs or chosen successor. Dhirubhai Ambani → Mukesh & Anil Ambani. Ensures business continuity.

🔴 Preparing for an Exit

6 Steps to Prepare for an Exit

  • Focus on the Business: Ensure the business is operating efficiently and remains profitable before exit. A well-managed, financially stable business attracts higher valuations.
  • Large & Diversified Customer Base: Multiple revenue streams reduce buyer risk. Avoid over-dependence on a few customers — this lowers valuation.
  • Regulatory Compliance: Follow all legal, financial, tax, and labour laws. Any compliance gap can delay or kill a deal.
  • Proper Documentation: Maintain up-to-date financial records — balance sheets, P&L, tax filings. Organised documentation builds buyer trust.
  • Review Contracts: Update supplier, customer, and employee contracts. Long-term client contracts increase business value significantly.
  • Competent Management Team: Train successors to take over smoothly. A business that depends entirely on the founder is worth less — buyers want continuity.

Enterprise Life Cycle & Lean Methodology Part A Guaranteed Question

🔴 Lean Business Model Canvas is marked as a "guaranteed question" — read thoroughly. Also know the 5 stages of growth, 5 Lean principles, 8 wastes, and Lean tools.
5 Stages of Business Growth
1. Start-UpBirth of enterprise. High risk, low revenue. Focus: product-market fit.
2. GrowthMarket adoption. Revenue rising fast. Focus: scaling operations.
3. ExpansionNew branches, product lines, geographies. Focus: managing complexity.
4. MaturitySales grow, but at decreasing rate. Profit starts declining. Focus: efficiency.
5. DeclineSales fall due to competition, changing behaviour, or disruption. Focus: pivot or exit.
Lean Methodology
Originated from Toyota Production System (TPS) in Japan. Adopted widely — from software (Agile) to healthcare to Indian manufacturing. Core idea: maximise customer value while eliminating all forms of waste.

5 Lean Principles

  • Value — Define what customers actually pay for
  • Value Stream — Map every step from raw input to final delivery
  • Flow — Eliminate stops, delays, and bottlenecks
  • Pull — Produce only when there's actual demand (JIT)
  • Perfection (Kaizen) — Continuous, never-ending improvement

8 Wastes (Muda)

  • Overproduction (making more than needed)
  • Waiting (idle time)
  • Transport (unnecessary movement of materials)
  • Over-processing (doing more than required)
  • Inventory (excess stock)
  • Motion (unnecessary worker movement)
  • Defects (rework, scrap)
  • Unused Talent (ignoring employee ideas)
🍪
Lean in Action — Indian Manufacturing
Parle Products (biscuits) applied Lean to cut waste by 40%
By installing IoT temperature sensors in ovens (defect prevention), using Kanban cards for raw material ordering, and applying 5S to production lines, Parle reduced biscuit breakage by 40% and cut warehouse storage costs by placing production closer to demand cycles. Production efficiency rose 35%. JIT delivery means fresher product reaches retailers.
🔧 Lean Tools: 5S (Sort · Set in Order · Shine · Standardize · Sustain) · Kanban (visual workflow) · Kaizen (continuous improvement) · VSM — Value Stream Mapping · JIT — Just-in-Time production.

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit V

Q: Distinguish between Angel Investor and Venture Capitalist.
Angel = HNI investing own money; seed stage; fast decisions; low control; informal mentorship. VC = institutional firm managing others' money; board seat; ≤50% equity; portfolio approach; operational platform teams.
Q: What is a GI Tag? Give 2 Indian examples.
Geographical Indication — protects products with qualities or reputation unique to a specific place. Indian examples: Darjeeling Tea, Kanjeevaram Silk, Basmati Rice, Alphonso Mango (any 2).
Q: What are the 5 stages of the Enterprise Life Cycle?
Start-Up → Growth → Expansion → Maturity → Decline. Each stage requires a different strategic focus.
Q: What is Kaizen?
Japanese for "change for better." In Lean methodology, it means continuous small improvements to processes by everyone in the organisation — not just management. Toyota's famous suggestion system receives 1 million employee ideas per year.

UNIT I · 5 HRS

Introduction to Entrepreneurship

Definitions, traits, types, and the role of entrepreneurs in economic growth.

Definitions Part A

Entrepreneur (Schumpeter): "An innovator who carries out new combinations to initiate economic development through new products, markets, raw materials, and organisations."
Entrepreneur (Peter Drucker): "A person who always searches for change, responds to it and exploits it as an opportunity."
Entrepreneurship: The dynamic process of vision, change, and creation requiring energy and passion toward implementation of new ideas and creative solutions.
Enterprise: An organisation or activity — a framework within which business decisions are undertaken.
★ Keyword: ENTREPRENDRE — French origin of the word "entrepreneur." Often asked as 2-mark Q.

Role of Entrepreneurs in Economic Growth Part B

  • Innovator: Introduces new ideas, products, services that disrupt/enhance markets
  • Employment Generation: Creates jobs, reduces unemployment
  • Economic Growth: Contributes to GDP, increases production & consumption
  • Social Stability: Reduces poverty, bridges income gaps
  • Balanced Regional Development: Sets up industries in less-developed areas
  • Export Promotion: Creates globally competitive goods for export
  • Import Substitution: Produces quality alternatives to imports domestically

Sources of Business Ideas Part A

  • Human Needs & Wants: Tiffin services, BigBasket, Blinkit
  • Solving Social Problems: Ola/Uber (transport), Byju's (education), Practo (health)
  • Improving Existing Products: Rebel Foods cloud kitchens, Paytm/PhonePe

Opportunity Analysis — Two-Stage Process Part B

💡 Peter Drucker: "The overwhelming majority of successful innovations exploit change."

Stage 1: Opportunity Sighting

  • Market Driven — Identifying unmet needs in existing markets
  • People Driven — Leveraging personal skills/passion
  • Analysis of trends, gaps, inefficiencies

Stage 2: Opportunity Evaluation

  • Using Opportunity Evaluation Sieve
  • 3 perspectives: Entrepreneur, Customer, Industry
  • Screening for feasibility & viability
3 Evaluation Perspectives
1. Entrepreneur's PerspectiveDoes it align with my passion, skills, values, risk appetite?
2. Customer/Market PerspectiveReal customer need? Adequate market size? Cultural alignment?
3. Industry/Business PerspectiveCompetition structure? Sustainable competitive advantage? Profitability?

What Defines a Good Opportunity? Part A

  • Solves a real problem
  • Large enough customer base to be viable
  • Minimal regulations / no monopoly
  • Ability to differentiate from competition

UNIT III · 5 HRS

Business Models & Business Creation

Types of business models, entity structures, setting up a business, and government policies.

Business Model — Meaning Part A

A business model is the strategy a company uses to generate revenue. It describes how an organisation creates, delivers, and captures value.

Types of Business Models Part C

1
Niche — Targets a small, specific customer segment with unique needs. Can charge premium. Example: Vegan Blue Shoes (vegan footwear).
2
Long Tail — Coined by Chris Anderson (2004). Selling large number of unique items in small quantities. "Head" = bestsellers; "Tail" = niche products that collectively rival bestsellers. Examples: Amazon, Netflix, Spotify, YouTube.
3
Mass Customisation — Combines mass production efficiency with individual customisation. Examples: Nike (Nike By You), car manufacturers with custom options.
4
Freemium — Basic version free; charge for advanced features. Types: Feature Limited (Canva), Time Limited (Netflix trial), Capacity Limited (Dropbox), Customer Class Limited. Examples: Skype, LinkedIn, Spotify.
5
Unbundling — Breaking a product into standalone components, sold separately. Examples: Tata Sky (channel packs), budget airlines (base fare + baggage fees separate).
6
Bundling — Offering several products as a combined package. Works with economies of scale, low marginal cost. Examples: McDonald's meal combos, Microsoft Office suite.
7
No Frills — Lowest price by removing non-essential features. Low costs drive low prices. Example: DMart (functional stores, everyday low pricing, bulk purchasing, owns properties).
8
Premium — High-end products for high-income consumers. High margin, lower volume. Brand image crucial. Examples: Rolls-Royce, BMW, Gucci, Rolex.
9
Open Business Model — Collaborates with external partners (customers, universities, suppliers) to create value. Example: Threadless (users submit & vote on T-shirt designs).
10
Multi-Sided Platform (MSP) — Connects two or more interdependent groups; grows via network effect. Examples: LinkedIn, Uber/Ola (connects drivers & passengers).
11
Social Enterprise — Business-minded solutions to social problems. Example: Amul (cooperative dairy).
★ Lean Business Model Canvas — One-page strategic plan focusing on Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, Value Proposition, Channels, Customer Segments, Cost Structure, Revenue Streams. Example: Uber's canvas.

Steps to Set Up a Business in India Part B

1
DIN (Director Identification Number) — Unique number from Ministry of Corporate Affairs. One person = one DIN. Valid for lifetime.
2
Digital Signature Certificate (DSC) — To electronically sign documents for ROC. Issued by agencies like TCS.
3
Reserve Company Name — Apply on MCA portal. Max 6 preferences; approved in 2–3 days.
4
Company Incorporation — File MOA + AOA with ROC. Prepared by CA/CS/Advocate. Requires common seal.
5
PAN — Permanent Account Number from Income Tax Dept. Essential for tax activities.
6
Shops & Establishments Act — Register to comply with labour laws.
7
GST Registration — Goods & Services Tax; enables collecting and remitting indirect tax.
8
Profession Tax — State government registration.
9
Provident Fund (EPFO) — Mandatory for 20+ employees. 12% from employer + 12% from employee on basic salary. UAN = 12-digit unique number.
10
ESI Scheme — For factories/establishments with 10+ employees earning ≤ ₹21,000/month. Employer: 3.25%; Employee: 0.75% of gross salary.

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit III

Q: What is the Long Tail business model? Who coined it?
A: Coined by Chris Anderson in 2004. Selling large number of unique items in small quantities; niche products collectively rival bestsellers. Examples: Amazon, Netflix.
Q: What is the difference between Freemium and Premium?
A: Freemium offers basic free + paid advanced features (Spotify, LinkedIn). Premium offers exclusively high-end products at high prices (Rolex, BMW).
Q: What is a network effect in Multi-Sided Platforms?
A: As the user base grows, the platform becomes more valuable. More drivers = better for riders; more riders = more income for drivers.
UNIT IV · 10 HRS

Building a Business Plan

Marketing plan, financial plan, organisational plan — the backbone of any new venture.

Business Plan — Overview Part A

A formal statement of business goals, reasons they are attainable, and a plan for reaching them. It describes all relevant external and internal elements of a new venture.

Why Write a B-Plan?

  • Support loan application
  • Raise equity funding
  • Define objectives
  • Agreement between partners
  • Evaluate new product/expansion

Contents of B-Plan

  • Executive Summary
  • Business Description
  • Product/Service Description
  • Market Analysis
  • Operational Plan
  • Sales & Marketing Plan
  • Financial Plan

Marketing Plan Part C

A written statement of marketing objectives, strategies, and activities to be followed in business plan.
1
Market Research — Collect, record, analyze market data; identify target customers, competitors, buying behaviour.
2
Target Market & Segmentation — Identify where product fits; find gap where competitors underperform.
3
Product/Service Strategy — Define product, market fit, added value above customer expectations.
4
Competition Analysis — Develop Unique Selling Proposition (USP); identify gaps vs competition.
5
Overall Business Strategy — State who you're selling to, what you're selling, what makes you distinct.
6
Market Strategies — Partnering & networking, advertising, promotions, internal communication.
7
Pricing, Positioning & Branding — Price strategy, market position, brand awareness.
8
Budget — Match strategies to budgets; determine in-house vs outsourced.
9
Marketing Goals — Quantifiable targets: e.g., 30 new clients, 30% income increase.
10
Monitor Results — Google Analytics, surveys, polls, database tools.
★ Marketing Mix (7 Ps): Product, Price, Place (Distribution), Promotion + Physical Evidence, People, Process.

Financial Plan Part C

Determines potential investment commitment and indicates whether the business plan is economically feasible. Includes the three primary financial statements.

Operating Budget

  • Detailed projection of income & expenses
  • Usually for a fiscal year/quarter
  • Roadmap for day-to-day operations

Capital Budget

  • Plan for fixed asset expenditures
  • Includes acquisition, building, repair costs
  • Part of annual business plan
Financial Statements
StatementShowsPeriod
Balance SheetAssets, Liabilities, Net WorthAt a point in time
Income Statement (P&L)Income, Expenses, Profit/LossOver a period
Statement of Retained EarningsChanges in retained earningsReporting period
Cash Flow StatementOperating, investing, financing activitiesOver a period
Assets = Liabilities + Net Worth (Shareholders' Equity)
Break Even Analysis
BEP = point where Total Revenue = Total Costs (TR = TC). Also called No Profit, No Loss Zone.
BEP (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
Types of Ratios
Liquidity RatiosAbility to meet current obligations
Solvency RatiosAbility to meet long-term obligations
Activity RatiosEfficiency of asset management
Profitability RatiosOverall efficiency / return on value
Elements of Cost

Direct Costs (Prime Cost)

  • Direct Material (timber in furniture)
  • Direct Labour (carpenter's salary)
  • Direct Expenses (transport of raw materials)

Indirect Costs (Overheads)

  • Indirect Material (lubricating oil, nails)
  • Indirect Labour (supervisor, watchman)
  • Indirect Expenses (admin, selling expenses)

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit IV

Q: What is Break Even Point?
A: The point where Total Revenue = Total Costs (no profit, no loss). Formula: BEP = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost).
Q: State the difference between MOA and AOA.
A: MOA defines company's relationship with outsiders and scope (6 clauses). AOA defines internal rules and management procedures.
Q: List the 7 Ps of Marketing Mix.
A: Product, Price, Place, Promotion, Physical Evidence, People, Process.

Growth Strategies Part B

Internal Growth

  • Market Penetration (sell more of same)
  • Market Development (new markets)
  • Product Development (new products for existing customers)
  • Diversification (add new products/markets)

External Growth

  • Joint Venture — Temporary partnership for specific goal
  • Merger — Absorption or Amalgamation
  • Sub-contracting — Assign work to another firm
  • Franchising — License trademark + systems; franchisee pays fee + royalty
Merger types: Absorption = one company takes over another (acquired loses identity). Amalgamation = two companies form a completely new company (both cease to exist).

Exit Strategies Part B

  • Merger or Acquisition (M&A) — Selling to another company; merger = combine; acquisition = one buys another
  • Buyout — Transfer to partners, employees (MBO) or external investors
  • IPO (Initial Public Offering) — Take company public; sell shares on stock market
  • Liquidation — Close business; sell assets to repay debts
  • Family/Successor Handover — Pass to heirs/chosen successor for business continuity

Enterprise Life Cycle & Lean Methodology Part A

1. Start-UpBirth of enterprise
2. GrowthAdopted by market
3. ExpansionBranches, new product lines
4. MaturitySlowing growth, profit decline
5. DeclineCompetition/innovation disruption
Lean Methodology
Originated from Toyota Production System (TPS). Focuses on maximising value while minimising waste.

5 Key Principles

  • Value (what customer pays for)
  • Value Stream (map the process)
  • Flow (no bottlenecks)
  • Pull (demand-driven production)
  • Perfection / Kaizen (continuous improvement)

8 Wastes (Muda)

  • Overproduction
  • Waiting
  • Transport (unnecessary)
  • Over-processing
  • Inventory (excess)
  • Motion (extra worker movement)
  • Defects
  • Unused Talent
🔧 Lean Tools: 5S (Sort, Set, Shine, Standardize, Sustain), Kanban, Kaizen, Value Stream Mapping (VSM), Just-in-Time (JIT).

🎯 Quick Exam Q&A — Unit V

Q: Difference between Angel Investor and Venture Capitalist.
A: Angel = HNI investing own money in very early stage; low control; informal mentorship. VC = institutional firm managing others' money; takes board seat; invests at early-growth stage; portfolio approach.
Q: What is a GI Tag? Give 2 examples.
A: Geographical Indication — protects products with qualities unique to a specific place. Examples: Darjeeling Tea (India), Champagne (France).
Q: What are the 5 stages of the Enterprise Life Cycle?
A: Start-Up → Growth → Expansion → Maturity → Decline.
Q: What is an IPO as an exit strategy?
A: Initial Public Offering — the company goes public by selling shares on the stock market, allowing the founder to exit with returns.

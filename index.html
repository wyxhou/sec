Definitions & Key Concepts Part A

★ Etymology: "Entrepreneur" comes from the French word ENTREPRENDRE (to undertake). Very common 2-mark question.
An innovator who carries out new combinations to initiate the process of economic development — through introduction of new products, new markets, conquest of new sources of raw material and establishment of new organisation or industry. — J. A. Schumpeter
A person who always searches for change, responds to it, and exploits it as an opportunity. Innovation is the basic tool of entrepreneurs, the means by which they exploit change as an opportunity for a different business or service. — Peter Drucker
An entrepreneur is opportunity-obsessed, with the goal of creating value, without regard to resources currently controlled. — Stanford University
Entrepreneurship: The dynamic process of vision, change, and creation. It requires application of energy and passion towards the creation and implementation of new ideas and creative solutions.
Enterprise: An organisation or activity that is a place of business for an entrepreneur. An undertaking or adventure involving uncertainty and risk and requiring innovation. A framework within which the decisions of the business are undertaken. It consists of people who work together, utilises resources (raw material, machinery, human resources), and is a continuing entity.

Functions of an Entrepreneur Part B High Priority

🔴 Sometimes asked as a case study. Characteristics describe what an entrepreneur is; functions describe what they do — do not mix them up.

Core Functions

  • Innovation: Introducing new products, services, methods, or markets. Schumpeter called this "creative destruction."
  • Risk-Bearing: Willingly taking on financial, social, and psychological uncertainty of a new venture.
  • Organising Resources: Combining land, labour, capital, and technology into a productive enterprise.
  • Decision-Making: Making key strategic choices about production, pricing, markets, and investments.
  • Management & Coordination: Planning, organising, directing, and controlling business operations.
  • Marketing: Identifying customer needs and ensuring the product reaches the right people at the right price.
  • Capital Formation: Mobilising funds from savings, banks, investors, or government schemes.
  • Employment Creation: Creating jobs directly (within the firm) and indirectly (through the supply chain).
⚠️ Case Study Tip: If asked as a case, identify which function applies — e.g., Deepinder Goyal pivoting Zomato during COVID = Decision-making + Innovation.

Locus of Control Part A

Locus of Control (Julian Rotter, 1954): A person's belief about what causes outcomes in their life — internal (own actions) or external (luck, others, fate).

Internal Locus of Control

  • Believes outcomes result from personal effort and ability
  • Takes responsibility for success and failure
  • Proactive, self-directed, high achievement drive
  • Most successful entrepreneurs have this
  • Example: "I failed because I didn't prepare enough."

External Locus of Control

  • Believes outcomes are controlled by fate, luck, or others
  • Passive, dependent, lower initiative
  • Less likely to take entrepreneurial risks
  • Example: "I failed because the market was unfair."
💡 Why It Matters: Entrepreneurs need a strong internal locus — they must believe their decisions shape outcomes, even in uncertain markets. This drives persistence, initiative, and resilience.

Role of Entrepreneurs in Economic Development Part B

  • Role as an Innovator: Entrepreneurs drive economic growth by introducing new ideas, products, and services that disrupt and enhance existing markets. Jio reduced data prices by 95% overnight.
  • Generation of Employment Opportunities: By establishing and expanding businesses, entrepreneurs create jobs. MSMEs provide 110 million jobs in India.
  • Complementing & Supplementing Economic Growth: Entrepreneurs contribute to GDP by fostering industrial growth and increasing production and consumption.
  • Role in Bringing About Social Stability: By creating economic opportunities, entrepreneurs help reduce poverty, bridge income gaps, and foster societal well-being.
  • Role in Balanced Regional Development: Entrepreneurs help reduce regional disparities by setting up industries in less developed areas (MSME clusters — Tiruppur, Moradabad, Rajkot).
  • Role in Export Promotion: Entrepreneurs enhance a nation's foreign exchange earnings. MSME products = 45.73% of India's total exports (2023-24).
  • Import Substitution: Entrepreneurs reduce dependency on imports by producing quality alternatives domestically (Sun Pharma generic medicines, Indian semiconductor initiative).

Characteristics describe what an entrepreneur IS (e.g., creative, persistent, risk-taker). Functions describe what an entrepreneur DOES (e.g., organises resources, bears risk, creates employment). Never mix these up in answers.

🧠 The Idea vs Opportunity Test
"I should make an app for finding parking" = Idea. "10 million urban commuters spend 18 minutes daily finding parking, no dominant solution exists, municipalities are willing to partner" = Opportunity. The difference is evidence.
What defines a good opportunity? Solving a real problem · A large enough number of customers to make the business viable · Minimal regulations or no monopoly · The ability to differentiate your product to maintain competitive advantage.
⚠️ How Good Ideas Go Bad: Novice entrepreneurs are often fascinated with their ideas whereas experienced entrepreneurs are more practical. The need to get the market right is critical — if you get the market wrong, no idea can save you. Picking the right business partners is also important.

Opportunity Analysis — Two Stages Part B High Priority

🔴 "Two stages of opportunity selection" is explicitly flagged. Know the names, definitions, and sub-types (Market-Driven vs People-Driven) clearly.
As the starting point of business innovation an entrepreneur should 'Identify and Analyse Opportunity'. The process of opportunity selection has two stages: Opportunity Sighting and Opportunity Evaluation.
The overwhelming majority of successful innovations exploit change. — Peter F. Drucker
Stage 1: Opportunity Sighting
Opportunity is something which arises in the marketplace as a result of some action or change in the social domain. It can be classified into two types:

Market-Driven Opportunities

  • Focus: Identifying unmet needs or under-served segments within existing markets
  • Approach: Analysing market trends, competitive landscapes, and customer behaviours to spot gaps or inefficiencies
  • Example: Developing a new more efficient delivery service for online retailers
  • Example: Creating a subscription box for organic pet food
  • Example: Zepto (10-min grocery — born from frustration with slow delivery)

People-Driven Opportunities

  • Focus: Leveraging unique skills, passions, or personal experiences to create a business
  • Approach: Identifying problems that the entrepreneur is uniquely positioned to solve
  • Example: A chef opening a restaurant specialising in grandmother's traditional recipes
  • Example: A graphic designer launching a freelance branding business
  • Example: A former teacher creating online courses for homeschooling families
💡 Many successful businesses combine elements of both market-driven and people-driven approaches. Identifying and evaluating both types is crucial for making informed entrepreneurial decisions.
Stage 2: Opportunity Evaluation
The Opportunity Evaluation Sieve is a tool used to systematically assess the viability and potential of new business opportunities. It helps filter out less promising ideas and focus on those with the highest potential for success. Basing the business on a valid and feasible idea — opportunity map — is the best possible way to build a successful and sustainable business.

Questions to Ask When Screening a Venture Opportunity Part B High Priority

🔴 "Questions to ask when screening" are explicitly flagged — usually asked as "A good idea is not necessarily a good business opportunity. Justify."
4 Key Screening Questions
1
Who is your target customer? Understanding your ideal customer is paramount. This involves demographics (age, gender, location, income), psychographics (lifestyle, interests, values), and their specific needs and pain points.
Example: A company launching organic skincare might target urban, millennial women who are health-conscious and willing to pay a premium for natural ingredients.
2
How big is your market? Market size analysis helps determine the potential revenue and growth of your business. Consider market segmentation, market share, and market trends.
Example: A chain of budget hotels in tier-2 cities would research the middle-class population size and existing supply of budget accommodations.
3
What is your business model? Your business model outlines how you will create, deliver, and capture value. Key elements include revenue streams, cost structure, value proposition, and competitive advantage.
Example: A food delivery startup might employ a subscription model for regular customers, offering discounts and exclusive deals.
4
How can you protect your business? Intellectual property protection, competitive advantage, and risk management are crucial for long-term success.
Example: A software company developing a unique AI-powered solution might seek a patent to protect its intellectual property.

Business Models — Meaning & Types Part C High Priority

🔴 Types of business models are flagged. Know all types with Indian examples.
A business model is the strategy that a company uses to generate revenue from its product or service offering. It describes the rationale of how an organisation creates, delivers, and captures value. It is a set of planned activities designed to result in profit in a marketplace — specifying the company's position in the value chain.
Types of Business Models
🎯
1. Niche
Targets a small, highly defined customer group with unique needs, preferences, or problems. Able to charge premium price due to compatibility of niche needs and product features. Instead of being broad like Amazon, a niche business targets a specific segment.

2. Long Tail
Coined by Chris Anderson (2004). Strategy of selling a large number of unique items in relatively small quantities. Products with low demand can collectively make up a market share rivalling bestsellers — but only if the store or distribution channel is large enough. The head = small number of bestsellers; The long tail = very large number of niche products.

3. Mass Customisation
Combines the low unit costs of mass production processes with the flexibility of individual customisation. Factory-scale efficiency with personal-scale experience.

4. Freemium
Offers a basic version free and charges a premium for advanced features, functionality, or extended use. Four subtypes: Feature Limited (Canva), Time Limited (Netflix 30-day trial), Capacity Limited (Dropbox 2 GB free), Customer Class Limited (different levels for students vs enterprises).

5. Unbundling
Breaks up a product into independent components and sells each separately. Upsells add-ons. Customers pay only for what they use. Steps: Break up product into independent components → Sell components as standalone → Strip away add-ons → Upsell add-ons.

6. Bundling
Offers several products for sale as a combined product. Works well when: economies of scale in production, low marginal cost of bundling, high customer acquisition costs, and consumers appreciate simplification.

7. No Frills
Offers product or service at lowest possible price by removing non-essential features. Low prices driven by low costs. Simplified offerings (basic core product without extras), cost-efficient operations (fewer staff, minimal ambience, bulk sourcing), and everyday value pricing.
DMart (owns store properties, bulk buying directly from manufacturers, limited advertising)Udupi restaurants

8. Premium
Offers high-end products appealing to high-income consumers. Brand image is an important factor. Seeks higher profit margin on lower sales volume. Quality is often a subjective matter.

9. Open Business Model
Promotes access to the end product's source materials. The company creates and captures value by collaborating with external partners (customers, developers, universities, even competitors) to co-create value. Advantages: problems explored at little cost, taps wider range of talent, first-hand customer insight, builds brand community.

10. Multi-Sided Platform (MSP)
Connects two or more interdependent groups of customers and enables them to interact or transact with each other. As the market grows in number, it also grows in value — this is the Network Effect.

11. Social Enterprise
Works on business-minded solutions to social problems. Social value is the primary goal; revenue is a means, not the end.

Lean Business Model Canvas Part C Guaranteed ★

🔴 Guaranteed question — read this section thoroughly. One-page plan for startups. Adapted by Ash Maurya from Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas.
The Lean Business Model Canvas is a one-page strategic plan that helps startups and innovators test, pivot, and refine their business model quickly by focusing on problems, solutions, and learning instead of long predictions. Before using it, define: Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, Value Proposition, Channels, Customer Segments, Cost Structure, and Revenue Streams.
9 Components (use Uber as the running example)
1
ProblemTop 3 problems your customer faces. Having a shared understanding of the problem is key. What is the problem? Who does it affect? How does your solution specifically address the problem's root?
Uber: Taxis are expensive, unreliable, and hard to find.
2
Customer SegmentsWho has this problem? Define them as tightly as possible. Who is your ideal customer? Who is your solution targeted towards?
Uber: Urban commuters, business travelers, event-goers.
3
Unique Value Proposition (UVP)A single, clear, compelling message that states why you are different and worth attention. What makes your solution special relative to the ecosystem of other solutions? How do you stand out?
Uber: "Tap a button, get a ride." Fast, affordable, and convenient.
4
SolutionTop features that solve the listed problems. What does your product/service accomplish? How does it provide a unique solution?
Uber: Mobile app that connects riders with nearby drivers via GPS.
5
ChannelsHow you reach your customer segments — existing customers and potential new users. Which channels of communication will you use? How will you get people's attention?
Uber: Mobile app (iOS/Android), word of mouth, digital ads.
6
Revenue StreamsHow will your company create revenue and make a profit? Per transaction, subscription, or freemium?
Uber: Commission on rides (20–25% of fare).
7
Cost StructureWhat is the pricing model? What are fixed and variable costs?
Uber: App development, server costs, driver incentives, marketing, insurance.
8
Key MetricsWhat are the numbers that tell you if your business is working? How will you measure what is successful?
Uber: Number of rides per day, customer acquisition cost, driver availability, churn rate.
9
Unfair AdvantageWhat do you have that cannot be easily copied or bought? Network effects, proprietary data, founder expertise, regulatory moat.
Uber: City-level driver network density — once established, nearly impossible for a new entrant to replicate quickly.

Usable OnlineEntire teams can collaborate on it in real-time using online whiteboards
VisualisationVisual elements make understanding and collaboration easier than text-based models

Forms of Business Organisation — Entity Types Part C High Priority

🔴 Focus on Sole Proprietorship, Joint Stock Company, LLC, and Cooperative — including advantages, disadvantages, and liability type.
Sole Proprietorship Unlimited Liability
An unincorporated business owned by an individual. Not a separate legal entity — owner IS the business. Individual receives all profits or losses. Sole proprietorships represent the largest number of businesses in most countries but typically the smallest in size.
✓ Cheap setup, few government regulations, pays individual income tax (avoids corporation tax)
✗ Unlimited liability (personal assets at risk), failure to raise funds, short life of business, lack of business skills
e.g. A local kirana store owner
Partnership Unlimited Liability
Two or more people pool resources, skills, and capital. Operates as a collection of sole proprietorships. Partners share profits and losses per an agreed formula. A partnership must be dissolved if ownership changes (partner leaves or dies).
✓ Cheap setup, more capital raised, reduced tax liability, combined skills
✗ Unlimited liability, short life of business, failure to raise capital, limit on number of partners
e.g. Law firms, CA firms
Joint-Stock Company Limited Liability
A business entity owned by shareholders. Each shareholder owns a portion proportional to their shareholding. Separate Legal Entity — can own property, sign contracts, sue or be sued. Shareholders can freely transfer shares. If bankrupt, creditors cannot touch shareholders' personal assets.
✓ Easy capital raising, perpetual existence, limited liability, shares freely transferable
✗ Complex setup, regulatory burden, public disclosure required
e.g. Reliance Industries Ltd. (shares on BSE/NSE)
Limited Liability Corporation (LLC) Limited Liability
A legal entity treated as a separate person from its owners. Owners are called Members. Ownership is held by percentage — transferring ownership requires consent of other members. Modern form of a joint-stock company emphasising owners' liability is limited to capital invested.
✓ Limited liability, flexible structure, less compliance burden than joint-stock
✗ Harder to transfer ownership, not suitable for large-scale public capital raising
e.g. Flipkart Pvt Ltd (before IPO)
Cooperative Limited Liability
A voluntary association started with the aim of service to its members. "All for each and each for all." Defined under Section 4 of the Indian Co-operative Societies Act, 1912. Owned by its members (not outside investors). Democratic — every member gets one vote regardless of capital contributed. Main aim is service to members, not profit maximisation. Surplus is partly distributed among members and partly reinvested.
e.g. Amul (3.6 million dairy farmers pool milk; profits shared based on milk supplied) · IFFCO (fertiliser cooperative) · Housing cooperatives, Credit cooperatives

Techniques of Market Survey

  • Observation: Researchers study consumer behaviour without direct interaction, capturing real-time actions, emotions, or responses. Helps in understanding customer preferences, decision-making, and engagement.
  • Experimentation: The experimenter deliberately manipulates or changes one or more variables so that its effect on other variables can be measured. E.g., A/B testing two different ad copies.
  • Depth Interviewing: "The administration of questions to respondents in a completely relaxed environment wherein they are free to express themselves without any fear of disapproval, dispute or admonition." Uncovers honest, nuanced opinions.
  • Projective Techniques: Used to uncover hidden emotions, attitudes, and motivations that consumers might not express openly. Respondents project their thoughts onto ambiguous stimuli, revealing subconscious feelings about products or brands.
  • Survey / Questionnaire: Structured collection of data from a sample of respondents. Most common primary data tool. Can be online, phone, or in-person.
  • Focus Groups: Small group discussion (6–10 people) moderated to gather qualitative insights on a product, brand, or concept.

Costing — Elements of Cost Part B Very Important — Asked Last Semester

🔴 "Elements of Cost" is explicitly marked as very important. Know the two categories (Direct / Indirect) with sub-types for each.
Cost: Consists of all the expenditure incurred in producing a commodity or executing an order. Elements of cost are: Material Cost, Labour Cost, and Expenses.

Material Cost

  • The cost of commodities supplied to an undertaking
  • Direct Material: Timber in furniture, cloth in dressmaking
  • Indirect Material: Lubricating oil, nails, screws, waste

Labour Cost

  • Expenses incurred on employees — wages, salaries, bonus, commission
  • Direct Labour: Salary to carpenter (directly involved in making the product)
  • Indirect Labour: Salary to supervisor, watchman, repairs and maintenance staff

Expenses

  • Costs not associated with material and labour
  • Direct Expenses: Cost of transportation of raw materials, hiring charges of machine
  • Indirect Expenses: Factory expenses, administrative expenses, distribution and selling expenses
Prime Cost = Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses
Overheads = Indirect Material + Indirect Labour + Indirect Expenses
Total Cost = Prime Cost + Factory Overheads + Office & Admin Overheads + Selling & Distribution Overheads
💡 Types of Overheads: Factory Overhead (indirect costs within the factory) · Office and Administrative Overhead (management, finance, HR costs) · Selling and Distribution Overhead (advertising, delivery, marketing costs).

Break Even Analysis Part B Practice Numericals

Break Even Analysis examines the interaction among fixed costs, variable costs, prices, and unit volume to determine that combination of elements in which revenues and total costs are equal. It gives the analysis of the sales volume where the variable costs and fixed costs of the products are recovered.
BEP (Break Even Point): The point where Total Revenue received equals the Total Costs associated with the sale of the product (TR = TC). Also called the No Profit, No Loss Zone. Below BEP = loss; above BEP = profit.
BEP (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
Core Distinction
Contribution / unit = SP - VC
BEP in Units
BEP (units) = FC / Contribution per unit
BEP in Sales Value
BEP (sales) = FC / P/V ratio
P/V ratio (Profit-Volume ratio) = Contribution / Sales.
☕
BEP Worked Example
Blue Tokai Café, Bengaluru
Fixed costs: rent ₹80,000 + staff ₹1,20,000 = ₹2,00,000/month. Each cup priced at ₹280, variable cost ₹80. Contribution per cup = ₹280 − ₹80 = ₹200. BEP = ₹2,00,000 ÷ ₹200 = 1,000 cups/month. Below 1,000 cups = loss; every cup above 1,000 = pure contribution to profit.

Financing New Ventures — Angel / VC / PE Part C High Priority

🔴 "Financing New Venture Capital" and "Funding options to raise startup capital" are flagged. Know the full comparison table: Angel vs VC vs PE.
Angel Investors
Angel Investors (also called informal investors, angel funders, private investors, seed investors, or business angels) are high-net-worth individuals who invest their own personal funds into early-stage startups in exchange for ownership equity or convertible debt. They are perceived as "filling the gap" between financing provided by family and friends and venture capitalists.

Key Characteristics of Angel Investors

  • Invest their own personal money (unlike VCs who manage others' money)
  • Invest in the entrepreneur starting the business rather than just the viability of the business
  • Focus on helping start-ups take their first steps, rather than possible profit
  • Provide more favourable terms compared to other lenders
  • Make decisions much faster than a VC committee (no bureaucracy)
  • Exert low control — rarely demand formal board seats
  • Provide operational support through informal mentorship, advisory, and by offering introductions to their personal network
  • To qualify as an Accredited Investor (SEC standard): net worth ≥ $1 million (excluding primary residence) OR annual income > $200,000 ($300,000 if filing jointly) for past 2 years
Venture Capital (VC)
Venture Capital (VC) is a form of financing provided by firms or funds to startups and emerging companies deemed to have high growth potential. Unlike traditional bank loans, VC is not debt — it is an equity investment, meaning the VC firm becomes a part-owner of the company.

Key Characteristics of Venture Capital

  • VC firms act as intermediaries between institutional investors (pension funds, endowments, wealthy individuals) and high-potential startups
  • Before investing, VCs perform exhaustive due diligence (financials, legal standing, team quality, market size)
  • A VC firm raises a "fund" with a specific lifespan (typically 10 years)
  • VCs invest in a "portfolio" of companies — knowing many will fail, hoping one or two will provide massive returns
  • Usually invest in 50% or less of equity (spread risk across portfolio)
  • Take a seat on the company's Board of Directors for high-level strategic oversight
  • VC and entrepreneur act as 'partners' — VC not only purchases equity but participates in management
  • Many VC firms have "Platform Teams" — specialists in marketing, HR, legal available to help portfolio companies at no extra cost
Private Equity (PE)
Private Equity (PE) firms are investment companies that buy and manage existing mature, established companies that are often underperforming or not making the profits they should due to inefficiency.

Key Characteristics of Private Equity

  • Raise capital from institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies, very wealthy individuals)
  • A typical fund lasts 10 years — first few years buying companies, later years selling them
  • Deeply involved in management — often replace the CEO or install their own team to fix inefficiencies
  • Use a small amount of own cash and borrow a huge amount of debt (Leveraged Buyout — LBO) using the target company's assets as collateral
  • Once they own the company, use the company's own cash flow to pay off the debt
  • Buy 100% ownership of the companies they invest in (unlike VC which buys ≤50%)

Growth Strategies — Sustainable Practices for Growth Part B Very Important ★

🔴 "Main strategies of growth — very important" per the important topics PDF. Know both Internal and External growth strategies with clear distinctions and Indian examples.
A growth strategy is a well-designed scheme meant to expand business operations, or a plan to help the enterprise grow in course of time. Two types: Internal (enterprises grow on their own without joining other enterprises) and External (enterprises grow by joining hands with other enterprises).
Internal Growth Strategies
1
Expansion — Market PenetrationSell more of the same product in the same market. Methods: advertising, discounts, better distribution. Example: Jio offering more data packages and low tariffs in the same Indian market.
2
Expansion — Market DevelopmentSell existing products in new markets — new locations or new customer segments. Example: Amul entering USA and international markets with existing dairy products.
3
Expansion — Product Development and/or ModificationIntroduce new or improved products for existing customers. Example: Amul adding ice cream, pizza to its dairy customer base.
4
DiversificationAdding more products, markets, or services to existing ones. Defined as the process of adding to the existing business in new directions. Example: ITC — from cigarettes to FMCG (Sunfeast, Bingo) to hotels to agribusiness.
External Growth Strategies
5
Joint VentureA restricted or temporary partnership between two or more firms to jointly achieve a specific goal. Profits and losses shared in agreed ratio. Usually formed for large projects.
Example: Maruti + Suzuki (Maruti Suzuki India Ltd.); Sony + Ericsson (Sony Ericsson)
6
MergerWhen two or more existing enterprises are combined to form a single entity. Two types:
• Absorption: One company takes over another. The acquiring company continues; the acquired company loses its identity.
• Amalgamation: Two or more companies combine to form a completely new company. All old companies cease to exist.
Why merge: increase market share, reduce competition, combine resources and technology.
Example: HDFC Bank + HDFC Ltd (2023) = Absorption. Zomato acquiring Blinkit = Acquisition.
7
Sub-ContractingA company (contractor) assigns part of its work to another company (sub-contractor). May include processing, transformation, or finishing of materials or parts at the request of the contractor.
Example: Zomato contracts logistics/delivery to third-party partners; large manufacturers subcontracting component production.
8
FranchisingArrangement where the franchiser grants the franchisee the right to use its trademark/trade-name and certain business systems and processes to produce and market a good or service according to certain specifications. Franchisee pays a one-time franchise fee plus a percentage of sales revenue as royalty.
Benefits: (1) Immediate name recognition, (2) Tried and tested products, (3) Standard building design and décor, (4) Detailed techniques in running and promoting the business, (5) Ongoing help in promoting and upgrading products.
Example: Domino's India = Jubilant FoodWorks as master franchisee; McDonald's India; DTDC courier franchise.

Lean Methodology Part B

Lean methodology is an approach to process improvement, product development, and business management that focuses on maximising value while minimising waste. It originated in the Japanese manufacturing industry, especially from the Toyota Production System (TPS), and later expanded into healthcare, software development, and startups.
5 Key Principles of Lean
1. ValueDefine what customers actually value — what they are willing to pay for. Anything that doesn't add value from the customer's perspective is waste.
2. Value StreamMap out the entire process (the "value stream") from idea to delivery. Identify which steps add value and which don't.
3. FlowEnsure work moves smoothly through processes without interruptions, delays, or bottlenecks.
4. PullWork should be driven by actual customer demand, not assumptions or forecasts. Produce only when there is demand — Just-in-Time (JIT).
5. Perfection (Kaizen)Continuously improve processes by eliminating waste, improving efficiency, and adapting to customer needs. Never-ending improvement.
8 Types of Waste (Muda)

8 Wastes

  • Overproduction — making more than needed
  • Waiting — idle time when processes are stalled
  • Transport — unnecessary movement of materials
  • Over-processing — doing more work than required
  • Inventory — excess stock that ties up resources
  • Motion — extra movement by workers
  • Defects — rework or scrap due to errors
  • Unused Talent — not utilising employee creativity and ideas

Lean Tools & Techniques

  • 5S: Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain — Workplace organisation
  • Kanban: Visual workflow management system
  • Kaizen: Continuous small improvements by all employees
  • Value Stream Mapping (VSM): Analysing the flow of materials and information
  • Just-in-Time (JIT): Producing what is needed, when it's needed
🏭 Biscuit Factory Example (from notes): A biscuit factory applied Lean by: (1) JIT supply from vendors, (2) Kanban cards for raw material replenishment, (3) Temperature sensors to prevent burnt biscuits (Quality at Source), (4) 5S workplace organisation, (5) Kaizen — workers suggested conveyor belts to reduce manual handling. Result: 35% increase in efficiency, 50% reduction in biscuit wastage, decreased inventory, faster delivery to distributors.

Exit Strategies Part B Very Important ★

🔴 Explicitly flagged as "very important." Know all 5 exit strategies + 7 reasons for exiting + 6 steps to prepare for an exit. Likely a Part B or Part C question.
An exit strategy is a planned approach to leave a business while maximising financial returns and minimising losses. It helps business owners transition out smoothly, whether due to profitability concerns, personal reasons, or strategic shifts.
5 Common Exit Strategies
  • Merger or Acquisition (M&A): Selling the business to another company for a profit. Merger = two companies combine. Acquisition = one company buys another. Example: Walmart acquired Flipkart for $16 billion. Zomato acquired Blinkit (formerly Grofers).
  • Selling to Investors or Management (Buyout): Transferring ownership to partners, employees, or external investors. Common in family businesses transitioning to professional management.
  • Initial Public Offering (IPO): Taking the company public by selling shares on the stock market. Founder dilutes stake for liquidity. Example: Nykaa, Zomato, and Paytm all used IPO as an exit/liquidity path in 2021.
  • Liquidation: Closing the business and selling all assets to repay debts. Used when the business cannot be salvaged. Example: Kingfisher Airlines (2012) — one of India's most famous liquidations.
  • Passing on to Family / Successor: Handing over the business to heirs or a chosen successor. Ensures continuity. Example: Dhirubhai Ambani → Mukesh and Anil Ambani; Bajaj Group succession.
7 Reasons for Exiting a Business
Lack of ProfitsBusiness fails to generate sustainable profits; continuing means operating at a loss
Loss of InterestEntrepreneurs sometimes lose motivation or passion — hard to sustain long-term commitment
Future ProspectsIndustry is declining or highly competitive — exit before business loses value (e.g., DVD rental → streaming)
Dissolved PartnershipDisagreements between co-founders on business direction leading to buyout or shutdown
Not Wanting Further RiskEntrepreneurs prefer financial security over risking further investment in uncertain markets
Other OpportunitiesOwners find better business or career prospects elsewhere; receive acquisition offers they cannot refuse
Personal ReasonsHealth issues, family commitments, lifestyle changes, or relocation force the owner to step away
🔴 6 Steps to Prepare for an Exit

Preparing for an Exit

  • Focus on the Business: Ensure the business is operating efficiently and remains profitable before exit. A well-managed and financially stable business attracts higher valuations from buyers.
  • Having a Large and Diversified Customer Base: A business with diverse revenue streams is more appealing to investors. Reducing dependency on a few customers mitigates risks and increases business stability.
  • Regulatory Compliances: Ensure the business follows all legal and financial regulations, including taxes, labour laws, and industry-specific rules. Any compliance issues could delay or complicate the sale.
  • Proper Documentation: Maintain clear and up-to-date financial records, including balance sheets, income statements, and tax filings. Organised documentation builds trust with potential buyers or successors.
  • Contracts: Review and update supplier, customer, and employee contracts to ensure legal validity. Long-term contracts with clients and vendors can increase business value for potential buyers.
  • Ensuring Leaving Behind Competent Management: Train and prepare key employees or successors to take over responsibilities smoothly. A strong leadership team ensures business continuity after the exit. A business that depends entirely on the founder is worth less.

Angel = high-net-worth individual investing own money; seed stage; fast decisions; low control; informal mentorship. VC = firm managing institutional money; board seat; ≤50% equity; portfolio approach; platform support teams; thorough due diligence before investing.

Q: What are the 5 stages of the Enterprise Life Cycle?
Start-Up → Growth → Expansion → Maturity → Decline. Each stage requires a different strategic focus — from survival and product-market fit at start-up to efficiency optimisation at maturity and pivot/exit at decline.
Q: What is Kaizen?
Japanese for "change for better." In Lean methodology, continuous small improvements to processes by everyone in the organisation — not just management. One of the 5 core lean principles (Perfection). Associated with Toyota Production System.
Q: Difference between Merger (Absorption) and Merger (Amalgamation)?
Absorption: One company takes over another — acquiring company continues to exist, acquired company loses its identity. Amalgamation: Two or more companies combine to form a completely NEW company — all old companies cease to exist. Example: HDFC Ltd merged into HDFC Bank (Absorption).
